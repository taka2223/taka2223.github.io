Flow-based Generative Model from Mean Field Perspective

Introduction

Flow matching and Schr$"{\rm o}$dinger Bridge Matching are methods for training generative models by matching the flow between two distributions through a neural network. The goal is to learn a mapping between two distributions (e.g. from Gaussian to natural images) by minimizing the difference between the pre-defined flow and the learned one. In this blog, we try to unify/ connect them from the perspective of mean field control (optimal control).

Flow Matching

Consider two distributions $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ over $\mathbb{R}^d$. The ODE based methods (e.g. normalizing flows, flow matching) learn a mapping between these two distributions by solving the following ODE:

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = f_\theta(x_t, t), \quad x_0 \sim \rho_0, \quad \rho_0=\mu_0, \quad \rho_1=\mu_1
\tag{1}
$$

where $f_\theta$ is a neural network parameterized by $\theta$. Once the velocity field $f_\theta$ is learned, we can sample from $\rho_1$ by solving the ODE:
$$
x_1 = x_0 + \int_0^1 f_\theta(x_t, t) dt,\quad x_0 \sim \rho_0, \quad \rho_0=\mu_0\tag{2}
$$

Construct A Path

Unlike normalizing flows, which only constraints the target distribution by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the generated distribution $\rho_1$ and the real target distribution $\mu_1$, flow matching methods do not directly minimize the KL divergence. Instead, they construct a path (flow) between the two distributions and fit the derivative (a.k.a. the velocity field) of the path by minimizing the following objective:
$$
\min_\theta \mathbb{E}{(x_0, x_1)\sim \pi}\left[\left|f\theta(x_t,t)-\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}\right|^2\right]\tag{3}
$$

where $\pi\in\prod(\mu_0,\mu_1)$ is one of the joint distribution whose marginals are $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$. $\Phi_t(x_0,x_1):\mathbb{R}^d\times \mathbb{R}^d\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d$ is the trajectory of the flow between $x_0$ and $x_1$ at time $t$. It satisfies $x_t=\Phi_t(x_0,x_1)$, and $\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}$ is the velocity field of the flow. The objective is to minimize the difference between the learned velocity field $f_\theta$ and the pre-defined velocity field $\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}$.

Choice of $\pi$ and $\Phi_t$

Here comes the choice of $\pi$ and $\Phi_t$. We first talk about the choice of the path $\Phi_t$ between two points $x_0$ and $x_1$. One of the most intuitive options will be the straight line, i.e., the linear interpolation $x_t=(1-t)x_0+tx_1$.

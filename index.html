Flow-based Generative Model from Mean Field Perspective

Posted on 2025-05-07 Edited on 2025-05-09

Introduction

Flow matching and Schr\(\ddot{\rm o}\)dinger Bridge Matching are methods for training generative models by matching the flow between two distributions through a neural network. The goal is to learn a mapping between two distributions (e.g. from Gaussian to natural images) by minimizing the difference between the pre-defined flow and the learned one. In this blog, we try to unify/ connect them from the perspective of mean field control (optimal control).

Flow Matching

Consider two distributions \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu_1\) over \(\mathbb{R}^d\). The ODE based methods (e.g. normalizing flows, flow matching) learn a mapping between these two distributions by solving the following ODE:

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{dx_t}{dt} = f_\theta(x_t, t), \quad x_0 \sim \rho_0, \quad \rho_0=\mu_0, \quad \rho_1=\mu_1 \end{equation} \]

where \(f_\theta\) is a neural network parameterized by \(\theta\). Once the velocity field \(f_\theta\) is learned, we can sample from \(\rho_1\) by solving the ODE: \[ \begin{equation}\label{ode} x_1 = x_0 + \int_0^1 f_\theta(x_t, t) dt,\quad x_0 \sim \rho_0, \quad \rho_0=\mu_0\ \end{equation} \]

Construct A Path

Unlike normalizing flows, which only constraints the target distribution by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the generated distribution \(\rho_1\) and the real target distribution \(\mu_1\), flow matching methods do not directly minimize the KL divergence. Instead, they construct a path (flow) between the two distributions and fit the derivative of the path (a.k.a. the velocity field) by minimizing the following objective: \[ \begin{equation} \min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{(x_0, x_1)\sim \pi}\left[\left\|f_\theta(x_t,t)-\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}\right\|^2\right] \end{equation} \]

where \(\pi\in\prod(\mu_0,\mu_1)\) is one of the joint distribution whose marginals are \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu_1\). \(\Phi_t(x_0,x_1):\mathbb{R}^d\times \mathbb{R}^d\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d\) is the trajectory of the flow between \(x_0\) and \(x_1\) at time \(t\). It satisfies \(x_t=\Phi_t(x_0,x_1)\), and \(\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}\) is the velocity field of the flow. The objective is to minimize the difference between the learned velocity field \(f_\theta\) and the pre-defined velocity field \(\frac{\partial\Phi_t(x_0,x_t)}{\partial t}\).

Choice of \(\pi\) and \(\Phi_t\)

Here comes the choice of \(\pi\) and \(\Phi_t\). We first talk about the choice of the path \(\Phi_t\) between two points \(x_0\) and \(x_1\). One of the most intuitive options will be the straight line, i.e., the linear interpolation \(x_t=\Phi_t(x_0,x_1)=(1-t)x_0+tx_1\). Hence, the velocity field is given by \(\frac{\partial \Phi_t}{\partial t}=x_1-x_0\).

Besides the simplicity of the linear interpolation, another advantage is that if the learned velocity field perfectly matches the pre-defined velocity field, then the generated distribution \(\rho_1\) will be exactly the target distribution \(\mu_1\) and we can sample from \(\mu_1\) by \(x_0+f_\theta(x_0,0)\) because the velocity is constant. ( Of course in most cases it can not fit perfectly and therefore we solve it with eq. \(\eqref{ode}\) ).
